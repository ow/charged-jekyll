<td class="padded" style="word-break: break-word;-webkit-hyphens: auto;-moz-hyphens: auto;hyphens: auto;padding: 0px 0px 10px;vertical-align: top;text-align: left;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;margin: 0;line-height: 19px;font-size: 14px;border-collapse: collapse !important;padding-left: 15px !important;padding-right: 15px !important;padding-bottom: 0px !important;"><h1 class="section-lead" style="padding: 0px;margin-top: 0px;margin-right: 0px;margin-left: 0px;line-height: 1.3;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: 700;margin: 0;text-align: left;word-break: normal;font-size: 20px;margin-bottom: 10px;"><a href="https://blog.twitter.com/2015/hearts-on-twitter" target="_blank" style="color: #222222 !important;text-decoration: none;">Twitter officially kills favorites for hearts</a></h1>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">After what seems like months of testing, Twitter made it official: the favorite is dead.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Twitter has made a big fuss about the replacement, because in its eyes it shows the company is executing on its vision and changing things that fundamentally make the social network easier to understand.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Instead, you&#39;ll now see a heart on tweets instead for &#39;liking&#39; them. I&#39;m not a fan, as I think it only offers a single plane of emotion to express, when I&#39;m often not &quot;liking&quot; something when pushing the favorite button on Twitter. A star could mean a lot of things because it was vague, but a heart is one thing: positive.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">What&#39;s interesting is as Twitter switches to a different emotion button, Facebook and Slack have already moved beyond to more complex emotions. I do think the move will give new users clarity about how to use the button, but it&#39;s <a href="http://www.buzzfeed.com/mathonan/rip-faves#.nl50PMPz9" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">nothing more than &quot;dude-fussing&quot;</a> as the company tries to look like its doing <em>something</em>.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Anyway. Stars are hearts on Twitter now, and a bunch of people that used Twitter for a long time got upset about it. Now, they basically all forgot.</p>

<p class="muted" style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #ABABAB !important;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;"><a href="https://blog.twitter.com/2015/hearts-on-twitter" target="_blank" style="color: #ABABAB !important;text-decoration: none;">blog.twitter.com</a></p>

<h1 class="section-lead" style="color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: 700;padding: 0;margin: 0;text-align: left;line-height: 1.3;word-break: normal;font-size: 20px;margin-bottom: 10px;"><a href="http://chrome.blogspot.ie/2015/11/chrome-os-is-here-to-stay.html" target="_blank" style="color: #222222 !important;text-decoration: none;">Google insists Chrome OS is here to stay</a></h1>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Following rumors last week that Google plans to merge Android and Chrome OS into a single platform for mobile, tablet and laptop, the company has come out swinging and reiterated that it&#39;ll continue to invest in Chrome OS.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Despite the WSJ report, Google says that there&#39;s &quot;there&#39;s no plan to phase out Chrome OS&quot; and that it&#39;s investing just as much resource as ever into the platform, pushing out new features and updates every six weeks.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">I&#39;d like to believe Google here, but it does have motive to sell this angle. The WSJ report pins the merge for well into 2017, which is a long way off, which would hurt Chromebooks in the short term and that project may never come to be -- Google is trying to mitigate that damage that could stop schools and large businesses adopting the OS.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">I don&#39;t have any further insider information, but I&#39;d be deeply surprised if Google <em>wasn&#39;t</em> looking to merge the two at some point, but I imagine the company considers it so experimental right now that in its eyes it&#39;ll not happen in any reasonable time frame.</p>

<h1 class="section-lead" style="color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: 700;padding: 0;margin: 0;text-align: left;line-height: 1.3;word-break: normal;font-size: 20px;margin-bottom: 10px;"><a href="http://www.wsj.com/articles/blackberry-priv-review-blackberry-may-win-you-back-with-android-1446786132" target="_blank" style="color: #222222 !important;text-decoration: none;">BlackBerry&#39;s new Android phone is looking promising </a></h1>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Wait... a BlackBerry phone people might actually want to use? It looks like it, with BlackBerry Priv on the horizon, the company has pulled out all stops to build something desireable.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Physical keyboards have this weird nostalgia attached to them, from a time before soft keyboards where it was -- what we remember through rose tinted glasses -- easier to type on than jabbing at a screen.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Reviews of BlackBerry&#39;s newest phone are out, and they&#39;re <a href="http://www.engadget.com/2015/11/06/blackberry-priv-review/" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">surprisingly positive</a>. The company built a device that&#39;s running almost-pure Android, but with <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2015/11/6/9680698/blackberry-priv-patch-android-security" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">some key enhancements that make it more secure</a>, and deliver reliable updates for owners.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Perhaps for iPhone users that were already considering the switch, this could be the real deal. It&#39;s an attractive enough phone that brings the physical keyboard back, from a company people know they can trust. BlackBerry is determined this will work, otherwise it&#39;s going to pull out of the smartphone business entirely.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">The thing I think the company is betting on is a nostalgia for physical keyboards, but I have a suspicion it&#39;s not as marketable as the company expects.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Soft keyboards are, in general, easier to type on and require less work. Now that they&#39;re normal and we&#39;re used to them, is there really any reason to go back? I&#39;m not sure I&#39;d be willing to - <a href="http://www.theverge.com/2015/10/27/9614604/priv-android-phone-hardware-future-of-blackberry" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">especially not for $699</a>.</p>
