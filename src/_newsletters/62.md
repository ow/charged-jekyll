<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Apple has its own router, why can&#39;t Google?</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">It announced its own WiFi router, called OnHub, that aims to make it easier to set up your new internet and can handle the incoming onslaught of &quot;smart&quot; devices for your home.</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">The OnHub has a number of features that make it easier to use than most routers, which are horrible to set up even for the technically minded. When you plug it in, there&#39;s an app that guides you through the steps in &quot;human speak.&quot; It also promises to tell you if there are any problems and makes it easier to share your password with visitors via the app.</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">My first thought when this was announced was that I don&#39;t want Google to own my router, but the more I thought about it, the more I realized that perhaps Google is one of the few companies I would be comfortable with trusting my internet connection to.</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Sure, Google is an advertising company, but it doesn&#39;t appear it&#39;ll be trying to inject ads into this and I trust it far more than I do my own internet provider&#39;s hardware or some random company like D-LINK with its crappy &quot;value add&quot; features. That said, I&#39;m not sure I see a reason to switch to this from an Apple Airport Extreme.</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">The most interesting selling point for the OnHub is Google plans to use it as the center of the &quot;smart home&quot; so your internet of things devices can connect seamlessly. It integrates the company&#39;s new &quot;<a href="http://www.engadget.com/2012/10/29/google-removes-nexus-q-store/" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">Brillo</a>&quot; smart device platform and can handle &quot;hundreds&quot; of devices simultaneously.</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">For $199 with AC wireless, the OnHub is a great option in the US, though my only concern is Google&#39;s hardware ambitions have been flippant at best. <h1 class="section-lead" style="color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: 700;padding: 0;margin: 0;text-align: left;line-height: 1.3;word-break: normal;font-size: 20px;margin-bottom: 10px;"><a href="http://www.wired.com/2015/08/cant-squat-spotifys-eerie-new-privacy-policy/" target="_blank" style="color: #222222 !important;text-decoration: none;">Spotify&#39;s privacy blunder</a></h1>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">If you visited any news site this week, you&nbsp;probably saw a bunch of <a href="http://www.wired.com/2015/08/cant-squat-spotifys-eerie-new-privacy-policy/" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">dramatic outrage</a> about how Spotify&#39;s new privacy policy is &#39;creepy&#39; and the company plans to steal your data.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">While the company did change its privacy policy today and it includes a number of weird additions such as a section that permits the upload of photos, the use of your phone&#39;s location and contacts and more.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">Spotify&#39;s real problem is that it&#39;s <a href="http://thenextweb.com/opinion/2015/08/21/spotifys-problem-isnt-privacy-its-being-terrible-at-communication/" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">failing to justify why it&#39;s doing these things</a> -- it <a href="https://news.spotify.com/us/2015/08/17/updated-terms/" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">published a blog post</a> explaining it, but if you read through the explainer you come away knowing less than when you started (or you just start falling asleep like I did).</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">This kind of problem occurs repeatedly, because big companies don&#39;t mull their privacy policies down to something actual humans can read. Spotify was too broad in its policy and explanation, for example:</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">&quot;With your permission, we may collect information stored on your mobile device, such as contacts, photos, or media files.&quot;</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">On first read, that sounds scary. I reacted the same way as everyone else did -- thinking that Spotify was just being creepy, when it actually really meant &quot;we&#39;ll use this data, when you allow us, to enable some features in our app.&quot;</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">If you&#39;re ever writing a privacy policy for your startup (or big company), step back and ask yourself if it&#39;s something actual humans can read. It&#39;s fine if you must have the legalese version of the policy, but write something that explains to other humans why you&#39;re taking certain data.</p>

<p style="margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;padding: 0;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">You can&#39;t rely on users to color between the lines -- they&#39;re not going to read through business speak to understand what you&#39;re truly saying, so avoid the drama entirely by making it clear.</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;"><a href="http://thenextweb.com/apple/2015/06/10/uh-oh-apple/" target="_blank" style="color: #D12026 !important;text-decoration: none;">Content blockers</a> are a new feature in iOS 9 that allow an app to tap directly into Safari and block advertising, trackers and other content before it loads anywhere in the system, even if it&#39;s within an app&#39;s web view.</p>

<p style="margin-left: 0px;padding: 0px;margin: 0;line-height: 25px;margin-bottom: 10px;color: #222222;font-family: &quot;Lato&quot;, &quot;Helvetica&quot;, &quot;Arial&quot;, sans-serif;font-weight: normal;text-align: left;font-size: 14px;">I&#39;ve been testing Crystal, one of these content blockers ahead of its release next month, and have been amazed by just how much better Safari performs and how fast web pages load, even on a 4G connection.</p>
